Spare Parts Modeling Assumptions

­¤ö¦ Repairable items

Behavior: A unit may fail multiple times during the period. Failed units are repaired and returned to service.
Model used: Poisson
Why: Failures may recur and are not limited to one per unit.
Examples: Pump, motor, gearbox, valve, compressor.

­¤öü Consumable items (replace on failure)

Behavior: Item is not repaired. Each failure consumes a new spare. The same position may require multiple replacements during the period.
Model used: Poisson
Why: Expected demand may exceed the installed base.
Examples: Fuse, filter, bearing, mechanical seal, belt.

ŌÜĀ’ĖÅ Non-repairable (single failure per unit)

Behavior: Each unit can fail at most once during the planning horizon. After failure, the unit is permanently replaced.
Model used: Binomial
Important constraint:
Maximum required spares Ōēż fleet size.
Why: A unit cannot generate more than one failure in the period. The total number of failures is physically limited by the number of installed units.
Examples: One-time cartridge, rupture disk, safety pin, single-use device.

­¤ōł Normal model (approximation)

Behavior: Used as a mathematical approximation of Poisson or Binomial demand when expected demand is large.
Model used: Normal (Gaussian approximation)
When valid: Expected demand is sufficiently high and variability is moderate.
Note: The Normal model does not change the physical assumptions of the item. It only simplifies calculations.
Examples: High-volume consumables, large fleets, long planning horizons.

Ōøö End-of-life / wear-out

Behavior: Reliability approaches zero within the planning horizon. Failure is almost certain for all units.
Model used: Deterministic (no probability)
Why: There is no uncertainty remaining; all units are expected to fail.
Examples: Life-limited components at end of service life.
